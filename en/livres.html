The book The Pianist, Wladyslaw Szpilman's account of his survival in the Warsaw ghetto. Other books on the same theme.

A literary work

The Pianist is originally an autobiographical account by Władysław Szpilman, a Polish pianist and composer of the Jewish faith. Jewish faith, who recounts how he survived the Nazi occupation of Warsaw and the genocide of the Jews. Published for the first time in 1946, the book enjoyed worldwide success thanks to its film adaptation by Roman Polanski in 2002. The film won three Oscars, including Best Director and Best Actor for Adrien Brody, who plays Szpilman on screen.

There are a few differences between the book and the film in the way Szpilman recounts the events of his life. For example, the film shows Szpilman playing the piano in an abandoned house, where he is discovered by a German officer, Wilm Hosenfeld, who comes to his aid. In the book, Szpilman does not play the piano at this point, but is surprised by Hosenfeld looking for food. The film also omits certain episodes from the book, such as Szpilman's suicide attempt Szpilman's suicide attempt after being separated from his family, or his friendship with another hidden Jew, Majorek.
