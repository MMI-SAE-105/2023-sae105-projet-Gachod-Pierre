The pianist and music

The film The Pianist dramatizes the passion and talent of Władysław Szpilman, a Polish-Jewish pianist who survives the the horror of the Warsaw ghetto and the Nazi occupation during the Second World War. Music plays an essential role not only as an artistic expression, but also as a symbol of resistance, hope and humanity. and humanity.

Among the musical works performed by Szpilman in the film, one of the most striking is the posthumous Nocturne n°20 in C sharp minor, opus posthumous, by Frédéric Chopin. This piece, composed in 1830, is dedicated to Chopin's Ludwika, as an exercise before beginning the study of his second concerto. It was not published until 1870, 21 years after the composer's death.

Nocturne n°20 is a solo piano piece of great beauty and expressiveness. It is characterized by a slow tempo (lento con gran espressione), a melancholy, lilting melody in the right hand, and harmonious arpeggios arpeggios in the left hand. It comprises two contrasting parts: the first, in C sharp minor, is soft and plaintive the second, in D-flat major, is brighter and more soothing. The piece ends with a return to the first part followed by a coda in C sharp major.

It appears at two key moments in the film. The first is at the start, when Szpilman plays the song on Polish radio as German bombs rain down on Warsaw. It's the radio station's last broadcast before its destruction. The second is at the end, when Szpilman plays the piece to a German officer, Wilm Hosenfeld, who discovers it in a ruined house. who discovers it in a ruined house. This officer, moved by the music and by the pianist's plight, comes to his aid and saves his life. and save his life.

Nocturne n°20 is thus a link between the beginning and end of the film, between life and death, between war and peace. It illustrates the power of music as a universal language, able to transcend borders, conflicts and suffering.
